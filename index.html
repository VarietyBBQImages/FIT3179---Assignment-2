THE LANGUAGES OF THE WORLD

The above map shows the total number of living languages used as first languages within countries, and the language diversity index of each country. The diversity index is defined as the probability of any two randomly selected people within a country to speak different first languages. Land-locked countries and densely packed island nations tend to have higher language counts and diversity indices. This is because these places allow for ease of travel between nations and therefore promote mixture between cultures. Whereas isolated island nations such as Iceland and Japan have low diversity indices as historically travel to these nations would have been much harder.
The bubble chart on the left displays the established vs. immigrant languages in countries around the world. The chart is interactive so you can zoom to view smaller nations. Established languages are languages which have existed in the country for the country's known history. Immigrant languages are languages which were brought to the nation through movement of people from other parts of the world. Modern western countries have high numbers of immigrant languages as higher standards of living attract people from around the world.

These two charts focus on the most highly spoken languages around the world. The bar chart shows total number of speakers and the number of countries which speak the language. For someone looking to learn a language they may want to take into account how many places they could speak that language, not just how many people speak it around the world. It's also clear how colonisation has had an effect on the number of places a language is spoken.
The tree map shows the proportion of people by percentage of speakers worldwide which speak the most popular languages around the world, hover to see which languages is which.
